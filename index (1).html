01 — The idea
A miniseries about tradition, charm, and the women who carry it forward
She already gathers people. She already curates the space. She already knows which flowers go on an Easter table and why. This series just puts a camera in the room.
The concept
A seasonal brunch and garden party miniseries, hosted by a rotating cast of real women across generations
Each episode is an intimate gathering — brunch, garden party, or afternoon tea — set in a beautifully curated home. The host either goes to a guest's space or welcomes guests to hers. Around the table, women share stories of tradition, celebration, and the rituals they've inherited or invented. The brand dresses the table. The women do the rest.
The setting
A real home, dressed through the Mary Square lens
Not a studio. Not a set. A real dining room, a garden, a back porch — curated with Mary Square product. The space is the campaign.
The conversation
Stories of tradition, passed down or newly made
What did your mother always do on Easter? What recipe do you make that no one else could? What does celebration mean in your family? Warm, specific, real.
The format
Miniseries — one episode per season, with variation
Spring brunch. Summer garden party. Fall harvest table. Holiday gathering. Each with its own palette, product story, and guest generation.
The throughline
Every generation of her customer, at the same table
The 35-year-old who is becoming the host. The 55-year-old who has always been. The 70-year-old who taught them both. One brand. One table. All of her.
02 — Why it works
Three reasons this series earns its place
It is authentic to the brand, it solves a real production problem, and it speaks directly to the women Mary Square already knows.
Reason one
It lives inside her identity, not outside it
Mary Square's customer doesn't need to be sold to through a lifestyle she aspires to. She is already living it. This series is a mirror, not a billboard. She sees herself at that table — and she wants to be the one who set it. That's not an advertising problem. That's an identity alignment. The content works because the brand already belongs in the room.
Reason two
It speaks to every generation of her — at once
The 35-year-old is learning to host. The 52-year-old has been doing it for decades. The 68-year-old is the one they all learned from. Each episode featuring a different generational guest is also a different ad set. The same production day produces assets mapped to every persona already established in the brand strategy. One shoot. Many audiences. Zero wasted footage.
Reason three
Tradition is culturally relevant — and undersupplied
There is a real appetite right now for content about slowing down, gathering, and doing things the way they used to be done. The pendulum is swinging back. Handwritten notes. Cloth napkins. A table worth sitting at. Mary Square is positioned at exactly the right moment to own this territory in a way no competitor is currently claiming.
03 — The format
What a production day actually looks like
Each episode is designed as a full-day production event in a single location, optimized to output content across every channel and use case simultaneously.
The space
A real home, dressed in Mary Square. Products styled naturally — not staged.
→
The gathering
3–5 women, one host, one generational theme.
→
The story
Conversation about tradition, celebration, what gets passed down.
→
The assets
Long-form, stills, Reels, UGC, paid creative — one day.
Host or guest
Two episode models
Model A: The series host comes to a guest's home — her space, her china, her garden. Model B: Guests come to the host — the space is styled fully in Mary Square. Both work. The mix keeps the series feeling alive, not formulaic.
Seasonal variation
Four episodes per year, each with its own identity
Spring: Easter brunch, new beginnings, pastels. Summer: Garden party, long table, outdoor living. Fall: Harvest table, layering, gratitude. Holiday: Intimate gathering, candlelight, tradition.
The product story
Every episode is a curated collection moment
The host selects a cocktail blend, a tea pairing, or a signature drink for the gathering. The table is set with Mary Square. The women wear it. The space is dressed in it. Nothing is labeled. Everything is seen. The product integration is total — and entirely natural.
04 — The personas
Every generation of her, at the table
The series is designed around the four generational personas already established in the Mary Square brand strategy. Each episode features one generation as the focal guest — and each episode's assets map directly back to her corresponding ad set.
The emerging host  ·  mid-30s
She is becoming who her mother already is
She is starting to host. She cares about getting it right. She buys the flowers, irons the cloth napkins for the first time, texts her mom to ask what she always put in the centerpiece. Her episode is about learning — and about the pride of the first really beautiful table she sets herself. She is Mary Square's growth customer. The series shows her what she's about to become.

She has been doing this for twenty years and she is very good at it
This is the heart of the Mary Square customer. She volunteers her home first. She has opinions about tablecloths. Her monogram is on the napkin rings. She doesn't need to be taught — she needs to be celebrated. Her episode is a tribute to the woman who shows up for everyone, beautifully, every time.

Her episode is about legacy — what gets passed down, what gets kept, what gets changed. She is the emotional anchor of the series and the highest-LTV customer in the ecosystem.

06 — The content system
One production day. Three asset classes. Every channel covered.
This is not a content series that sits alongside the marketing plan. It is the marketing plan. Each episode is an integrated production event that generates paid, organic, and UGC assets simultaneously — organized by persona so every asset already knows which ad set it belongs to.
Asset class 1
Editorial — owned & organic
Long-form episode video. Short-form Reels cuts. Behind-the-scenes. Still photography from the table. Quote cards from the conversation. All organic-native, all branded to the season.
Asset class 2
Campaign creative — paid
The production stills replace seasonal campaign shoots. Product in context. Women wearing the collection. The table dressed. Every frame is a campaign asset. Mapped by persona to the corresponding ad set.
Asset class 3
Ambassador & UGC — collection coverage
Guests become ambassadors. Their own content from the day — real, unscripted, shot on their phones — covers inbound collection pieces. The UGC layer is built into the episode structure.
The ad set architecture
Every asset is pre-tagged to a persona before the shoot happens
Because the series is organized by generational guest, each episode's assets map directly to an established persona ad set. The matriarch episode produces creative for the 60+ audience. The emerging host episode produces creative for the 35–45 audience. The confirmed host episode is the core set. Paid creative is not a post-production decision — it is built into the episode brief. One production day delivers a full quarter of persona-mapped paid assets.
The compounding effect
The series grows in value over time
Season one establishes the format. Season two deepens the relationship with returning guests and introduces new ones. The archive of content compounds — last spring's Easter table episode resurfaces this Easter. The asset library grows without additional shoots. The audience grows without additional spend. This is how a seasonal campaign becomes a brand asset.
07 — The budget case
This series is targeting the seasonal campaign budget — not adding to it
One production day structured as an episode delivers everything a seasonal campaign shoot delivers — plus long-form content, UGC, and an owned media asset that compounds. The campaign budget doesn't go away. It gets more work to do.

The net-new case
What the series produces that campaign shoots never did
A seasonal campaign shoot produces stills and maybe short video. It does not produce a long-form content library. It does not build an audience. It does not generate UGC. It does not create a brand asset that compounds in value across seasons. This series does all of those things on the same production day, at the same cost — or less, because the home location eliminates studio and set costs, and the guests participate without traditional talent fees.
08 — Next steps
What we need to make season one
Four decisions to move from concept to production.
01
Name the series
Select from the candidates on the cover or develop a new option. The name anchors the brand identity for the format — it should feel timeless, warm, and specific to her.
02
Define the host
Who is the consistent presence across all four episodes? An existing Mary Square ambassador, a creator who already lives this life, or a new face built for this series. She is the thread that makes it a franchise.
03
Map the seasonal campaign budget
Identify the current spend per seasonal shoot. That number becomes the episode production budget. Any delta between the two is the case for incremental investment — or the savings argument.
04
Greenlight episode one
Start with spring. One episode proves the format. One production day generates the first full quarter of persona-mapped paid assets, long-form content, and UGC. If it works, the series earns season two automatically.
The closing argument
She already sets the table. We just need to be in the room.
Mary Square already has the customer. She already lives this life. She already gathers, celebrates, and curates — beautifully, every time. This series doesn't ask her to become someone else. It asks her to sit down. And it asks the brand to set the table around her. That's not a content strategy. That's a brand homecoming.
